# Unit 1: Ecosystem Concepts

## 1.1 Environment And Environmental Studies

Environment means the total surroundings of an organism, including living and non-living components.

Environmental Studies is multidisciplinary. It combines:

- Biology: organisms, biodiversity, ecology
- Chemistry: pollutants, water/air chemistry
- Geography: landforms, climate, resources
- Economics: resource use, sustainable development
- Sociology and law: population, policies, awareness
- Engineering: pollution control, waste treatment, resource efficiency

MCQ trap: Environmental Studies is not only biology. It is multidisciplinary.

## 1.2 Segments Of Environment

| Segment | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Atmosphere | Air envelope around Earth | oxygen, nitrogen, CO2, ozone |
| Hydrosphere | Water bodies | oceans, rivers, lakes, groundwater |
| Lithosphere | Land/rock/soil layer | soil, minerals, mountains |
| Biosphere | Zone of life | plants, animals, microbes, humans |

MCQ trap: Biosphere is the life-supporting zone where atmosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere interact.

## 1.3 Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature where living organisms interact with each other and with the physical environment.

### Components

| Component | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Abiotic | Non-living factors | light, temperature, water, air, soil, minerals |
| Biotic | Living organisms | producers, consumers, decomposers |

### Biotic Components

| Type | Role | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Producers | Make food by photosynthesis | green plants, algae |
| Primary consumers | Eat producers | deer, rabbit, zooplankton |
| Secondary consumers | Eat herbivores | frog, small fish |
| Tertiary consumers | Top carnivores | tiger, eagle, shark |
| Decomposers | Break down dead matter | bacteria, fungi |

MCQ trap: Decomposers recycle nutrients; they do not produce food by photosynthesis.

## 1.4 Functions Of Ecosystem

Important functions:

- Energy flow
- Food chain and food web
- Nutrient cycling
- Ecological succession
- Productivity
- Decomposition

## 1.5 Energy Flow

Energy flow in an ecosystem is:

- Unidirectional
- From sun -> producers -> consumers -> decomposers
- Decreases at each trophic level

The 10 percent law says only about 10 percent of energy passes from one trophic level to the next. Most energy is lost as heat and metabolic activity.

Example:

Grass has 10,000 J energy.  
Grasshopper gets about 1,000 J.  
Frog gets about 100 J.  
Snake gets about 10 J.

MCQ trap: Energy flow is one-way, but nutrients are recycled.

## 1.6 Food Chain

A food chain is a linear sequence of who eats whom.

Example:

Grass -> Grasshopper -> Frog -> Snake -> Eagle

Types:

| Type | Starts With | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Grazing food chain | Green plants | grass -> deer -> tiger |
| Detritus food chain | Dead organic matter | leaf litter -> earthworm -> bird |

MCQ trap: A detritus food chain starts with dead organic matter, not living green plants.

## 1.7 Food Web

A food web is a network of interconnected food chains.

Why food webs matter:

- They make ecosystems more stable.
- They provide alternate food paths.
- They reduce dependence on a single food source.

MCQ trap: Food web is more realistic than a food chain.

## 1.8 Ecological Pyramids

| Pyramid | Shows | Usually |
|---|---|---|
| Pyramid of number | Number of organisms | May be upright or inverted |
| Pyramid of biomass | Dry weight/biomass | May be upright or inverted |
| Pyramid of energy | Energy at each trophic level | Always upright |

Most important MCQ fact: Pyramid of energy is always upright because energy is lost at each trophic level.

## 1.9 Ecological Succession

Ecological succession is the gradual and orderly replacement of one community by another over time.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Pioneer species | First species to colonize an area |
| Seral stage | Intermediate stage in succession |
| Climax community | Stable final community |
| Primary succession | Starts on bare area without soil |
| Secondary succession | Starts where soil already exists |

Examples:

- Primary succession: bare rock, new lava surface
- Secondary succession: abandoned field, burnt forest

MCQ trap: Secondary succession is faster because soil and seeds may already be present.

## 1.10 Reservoir Ecosystem

A reservoir ecosystem is an aquatic ecosystem associated with stored water, such as a dam, lake-like water body, or artificial reservoir.

Important features:

- Contains plankton, fish, aquatic plants, microbes
- Receives nutrients from surrounding land
- Affected by eutrophication, pollution, sedimentation
- Used for irrigation, hydropower, drinking water, fisheries

MCQ trap: In nutrient cycles, "reservoir" can also mean the main storage pool of a nutrient, such as atmosphere for nitrogen.

# Unit 2: Food Webs, Ecosystems, And Natural Resources

## 2.1 Forest Ecosystem

Forest ecosystem is dominated by trees and has high biodiversity.

Layers:

- Emergent layer
- Canopy
- Understory
- Shrub layer
- Forest floor

Functions:

- Carbon storage
- Soil conservation
- Water cycle regulation
- Habitat for biodiversity
- Climate regulation

MCQ trap: Forests reduce soil erosion by roots binding soil and canopy reducing rain impact.

## 2.2 Grassland Ecosystem

Grasslands are dominated by grasses, with few trees due to rainfall, grazing, or fire.

Examples:

- Prairies
- Savannas
- Steppes

Features:

- Moderate rainfall
- Grazing animals common
- Fire may maintain grassland
- Deep roots help soil fertility

MCQ trap: Grasslands are not "tree-less because soil is poor" only; rainfall and fire also matter.

## 2.3 Desert Ecosystem

Deserts have very low rainfall and organisms adapted to water scarcity.

Features:

- Low precipitation
- Extreme temperature variation
- Xerophytes common
- Sparse vegetation
- Low productivity

Plant adaptations:

- Thick cuticle
- Spines instead of leaves
- Deep roots or widespread shallow roots
- Water storage tissues

Animal adaptations:

- Nocturnal behavior
- Burrowing
- Water conservation

MCQ trap: Deserts can be hot or cold. Low rainfall is the defining feature.

## 2.4 Land As A Natural Resource

Land supports:

- Agriculture
- Forests
- Settlements
- Industry
- Biodiversity
- Mining

Major problems:

- Land degradation
- Soil erosion
- Desertification
- Deforestation
- Urbanization
- Mining damage

## 2.5 Soil Erosion

Soil erosion is removal of topsoil by wind, water, or human activity.

Causes:

- Deforestation
- Overgrazing
- Improper farming
- Construction
- Mining

Control:

- Afforestation
- Contour farming
- Terrace farming
- Strip cropping
- Check dams
- Controlled grazing

MCQ trap: Topsoil is most fertile, so its loss reduces agricultural productivity.

## 2.6 Desertification

Desertification is land degradation in arid, semi-arid, and dry sub-humid areas.

Causes:

- Deforestation
- Overgrazing
- Climate variability
- Excessive irrigation causing salinity
- Unsustainable farming

MCQ trap: Desertification does not only mean expansion of existing deserts; it means degradation of drylands.

## 2.7 Water Management

Important practices:

- Rainwater harvesting
- Watershed management
- Drip irrigation
- Recycling wastewater
- Reducing water leakage
- Protecting wetlands
- Groundwater recharge

MCQ trap: Drip irrigation is more water-efficient than flood irrigation.

## 2.8 Drought

Drought means prolonged deficiency of rainfall/water availability.

Types:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Meteorological drought | Rainfall below normal |
| Agricultural drought | Soil moisture insufficient for crops |
| Hydrological drought | Low surface/groundwater levels |
| Socio-economic drought | Water shortage affects society/economy |

MCQ trap: Meteorological drought is based on rainfall; hydrological drought is based on water bodies/groundwater.

# Unit 3: Pollution And Environmental Issues

## 3.1 Pollution

Pollution is the undesirable change in physical, chemical, or biological characteristics of air, water, or land that harms life or resources.

Pollutant: substance or energy causing pollution.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary pollutant | Emitted directly | CO, SO2, NOx, particulate matter |
| Secondary pollutant | Formed in atmosphere | ozone, PAN, photochemical smog |
| Biodegradable pollutant | Broken down by microbes | sewage, food waste |
| Non-biodegradable pollutant | Persists for long time | plastics, heavy metals |

MCQ trap: Ground-level ozone is a secondary pollutant; stratospheric ozone is protective.

## 3.2 Air Pollution

Major air pollutants:

| Pollutant | Source | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| CO | incomplete combustion | reduces oxygen transport in blood |
| CO2 | fossil fuel burning | greenhouse gas |
| SO2 | coal burning, industries | acid rain, respiratory irritation |
| NOx | vehicles, power plants | smog, acid rain |
| PM2.5/PM10 | dust, smoke, combustion | lung and heart problems |
| CFCs | old refrigerants, aerosols | ozone depletion |
| VOCs | paints, fuels, solvents | smog formation |

Control methods:

- Cleaner fuels
- Public transport
- Catalytic converters
- Electrostatic precipitators
- Scrubbers
- Green belts
- Emission standards

MCQ trap: Electrostatic precipitators remove particulate matter from industrial exhaust.

## 3.3 Water Pollution

Water pollution is contamination of water bodies that makes water harmful for use.

Sources:

- Sewage
- Industrial effluents
- Agricultural runoff
- Oil spills
- Thermal discharge
- Plastic waste

Important terms:

| Term | Meaning | MCQ Fact |
|---|---|---|
| BOD | Biological Oxygen Demand | High BOD means high organic pollution |
| COD | Chemical Oxygen Demand | Measures oxygen needed to chemically oxidize pollutants |
| Eutrophication | Nutrient enrichment | Causes algal bloom and oxygen depletion |
| Biomagnification | Increase of toxin concentration across trophic levels | Common with mercury, DDT |

MCQ trap: High BOD reduces dissolved oxygen and harms aquatic life.

## 3.4 Soil Pollution

Causes:

- Pesticides
- Fertilizers
- Industrial waste
- Heavy metals
- Plastic waste
- E-waste
- Biomedical waste

Effects:

- Loss of soil fertility
- Toxicity in food chain
- Groundwater contamination
- Reduced microbial activity

Control:

- Composting
- Bioremediation
- Organic farming
- Proper hazardous waste disposal
- Reduce chemical pesticides

## 3.5 Noise Pollution

Noise pollution is unwanted or harmful sound.

Sources:

- Traffic
- Construction
- Industries
- Loudspeakers
- Aircraft

Effects:

- Hearing loss
- Stress
- Sleep disturbance
- Hypertension
- Reduced concentration

MCQ facts:

- Sound is measured in decibels (dB).
- Noise above safe limits for long duration can cause hearing damage.

## 3.6 Nuclear Hazards

Sources:

- Nuclear power plants
- Nuclear weapons
- Radioactive waste
- Medical/research radiation sources

Effects:

- Cancer risk
- Genetic mutations
- Radiation sickness
- Long-term soil/water contamination

Control:

- Shielding
- Safe storage
- Monitoring
- Controlled disposal
- Emergency planning

MCQ trap: Radioactive waste can remain hazardous for very long periods depending on half-life.

## 3.7 Biomedical Waste

Biomedical waste is waste generated during diagnosis, treatment, or immunization of humans or animals.

Examples:

- Syringes
- Bandages
- Blood-contaminated materials
- Cultures
- Pathological waste
- Expired medicines

Risks:

- Infection spread
- Needle-stick injuries
- Toxic exposure
- Environmental contamination

Management:

- Segregation at source
- Color-coded bins
- Disinfection
- Incineration/autoclaving where appropriate
- Safe disposal

MCQ trap: Segregation at source is the first and most important step.

# Unit 4: Awareness, Laws, Climate Change, Ozone, Biodiversity

## 4.1 Environmental Awareness

Environmental awareness means understanding environmental problems and acting responsibly.

Important actions:

- Reduce, reuse, recycle
- Save water and energy
- Avoid single-use plastic
- Use public transport
- Plant and protect trees
- Participate in conservation
- Report pollution violations

MCQ trap: Awareness is not just knowledge; it includes responsible behavior.

## 4.2 Indian Environmental Laws - Must Memorize

| Law / Act | Year | Main Purpose |
|---|---:|---|
| Wildlife Protection Act | 1972 | Protect wild animals, birds, plants, protected areas |
| Water Prevention and Control of Pollution Act | 1974 | Prevent and control water pollution |
| Forest Conservation Act | 1980 | Restrict diversion of forest land for non-forest use |
| Air Prevention and Control of Pollution Act | 1981 | Prevent and control air pollution |
| Environment Protection Act | 1986 | Umbrella law for environmental protection |
| Public Liability Insurance Act | 1991 | Immediate relief for accidents involving hazardous substances |
| Biological Diversity Act | 2002 | Conservation, sustainable use, benefit sharing |
| National Green Tribunal Act | 2010 | Fast environmental justice and dispute handling |

Most common MCQ:

- Water Act: 1974
- Air Act: 1981
- Environment Protection Act: 1986
- Wildlife Protection Act: 1972
- Biological Diversity Act: 2002
- NGT Act: 2010

MCQ trap: Environment Protection Act, 1986 is called an umbrella act.

## 4.3 Climate Change And Global Warming

Global warming is the long-term increase in Earth's average temperature, mainly due to greenhouse gases.

Climate change is broader. It includes:

- Temperature rise
- Rainfall pattern changes
- Sea level rise
- Extreme weather
- Melting glaciers
- Ocean warming

Major greenhouse gases:

- CO2
- CH4
- N2O
- Water vapor
- Ozone
- CFCs/HFCs

Sources:

- Fossil fuel burning
- Deforestation
- Agriculture
- Industries
- Waste decomposition

Effects:

- Heat waves
- Sea level rise
- Biodiversity loss
- Crop yield changes
- Glacier melting
- More intense floods/droughts

Control:

- Renewable energy
- Energy efficiency
- Afforestation
- Public transport
- Waste reduction
- Carbon capture
- Sustainable agriculture

MCQ trap: Global warming is one part of climate change; climate change is the broader term.

## 4.4 Ozone Layer Depletion

The ozone layer is in the stratosphere and protects Earth from harmful UV-B radiation.

Ozone depletion is mainly caused by:

- CFCs
- Halons
- Carbon tetrachloride
- Methyl chloroform

Effects:

- Skin cancer
- Cataracts
- Reduced crop productivity
- Harm to phytoplankton
- Damage to materials

Important agreement:

- Montreal Protocol, 1987: phase-out of ozone-depleting substances.

MCQ trap:

- Stratospheric ozone is good.
- Tropospheric/ground-level ozone is a pollutant.
- Ozone hole is not the main cause of global warming; greenhouse gases are the main cause.

## 4.5 Biodiversity

Biodiversity means variety and variability of life.

Levels:

| Level | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Genetic diversity | Variation within species | rice varieties, dog breeds |
| Species diversity | Variety of species | plants, birds, insects in a forest |
| Ecosystem diversity | Variety of ecosystems | forests, deserts, wetlands |

Values of biodiversity:

- Consumptive value: direct use, e.g., food, fuel
- Productive value: commercial products, e.g., timber, medicines
- Social value: cultural/religious importance
- Ethical value: every species has right to exist
- Aesthetic value: beauty, tourism
- Ecological value: ecosystem stability, nutrient cycling

## 4.6 Biodiversity Conservation

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| In-situ conservation | Conservation in natural habitat | national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves |
| Ex-situ conservation | Conservation outside natural habitat | zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks |

MCQ trap: National parks and sanctuaries are in-situ conservation. Zoos and seed banks are ex-situ conservation.

## 4.7 Hotspots

Biodiversity hotspots are regions with:

- High species richness
- High endemism
- High threat level

India is associated with major biodiversity hotspots such as:

- Himalaya
- Indo-Burma
- Western Ghats and Sri Lanka
- Sundaland, including Nicobar region

MCQ trap: Endemic species are found naturally in a particular region and nowhere else.

# Key Comparison Tables

## Food Chain vs Food Web

| Food Chain | Food Web |
|---|---|
| Linear pathway | Network of food chains |
| Less stable | More stable |
| One organism usually has one main food link | Organisms may have multiple food links |
| Simple representation | More realistic representation |

## Primary vs Secondary Succession

| Primary Succession | Secondary Succession |
|---|---|
| Starts on bare surface | Starts where soil exists |
| No soil initially | Soil already present |
| Slower | Faster |
| Example: bare rock | Example: abandoned farmland |

## In-situ vs Ex-situ Conservation

| In-situ | Ex-situ |
|---|---|
| In natural habitat | Outside natural habitat |
| Protects whole ecosystem | Protects selected species/genetic material |
| National park, sanctuary | Zoo, seed bank |
| Better for ecosystem processes | Useful for critically endangered species |

## Global Warming vs Ozone Depletion

| Global Warming | Ozone Depletion |
|---|---|
| Rise in average Earth temperature | Thinning of stratospheric ozone |
| Mainly due to greenhouse gases | Mainly due to CFCs and halons |
| Causes climate change impacts | Increases UV-B exposure |
| Controlled by emission reduction | Controlled by Montreal Protocol |

10. Biomedical waste must be segregated at source.

13. Biomagnification increases toxic concentration at higher trophic levels.
14. Drought can be meteorological, agricultural, hydrological, or socio-economic.

8. b - 10 percent law explains energy transfer.
9. b - Soil already exists in secondary succession.

### Soil Science

Pedogenesis is the process of soil formation. It happens through weathering of rocks, addition of organic matter, movement of water, and activity of organisms.

Soil horizons:

| Horizon | Meaning | MCQ Point |
|---|---|---|
| O-horizon | Organic litter layer | Leaf litter, humus |
| A-horizon | Topsoil | Most biologically active and fertile |
| B-horizon | Subsoil / illuviation zone | Receives leached minerals, clay, iron, aluminium |
| C-horizon | Parent material | Weathered rock fragments |
| R-horizon | Bedrock | Unweathered rock |

Illuviation means deposition/accumulation of materials leached from upper layers. Therefore, B-horizon is called the illuviation zone.

Soil health risk:

- Soil may contain heavy metals, pesticides, pathogens, asbestos-like particles, or industrial chemicals.
- Eating or inhaling contaminated soil can cause poisoning, respiratory disease, or cancer risk.

|---|---|

| Epicenter | Point on Earth's surface directly above hypocenter |
| Focal depth | Depth of hypocenter below surface |
| Isoseismal line | Line joining places with same earthquake intensity |
| Magnitude | Energy released by earthquake |
| Intensity | Observed effect/damage at a place |

MCQ trap: Isoseismal lines show same intensity, not same magnitude.

### Succession Steps

Primary autotrophic succession usually follows this logic:

1. Nudation: formation of a bare area.
2. Invasion: arrival of propagules/seeds/spores.
3. Ecesis: successful establishment of species.
4. Aggregation: increase in number of individuals.
5. Competition and reaction: species modify the environment and compete.
6. Climax: stable terminal community.

MCQ trap: Ecesis is establishment, not arrival. Arrival is part of invasion.

### Municipal Solid Waste And Biomedical Waste

Municipal solid waste can cause:

- Air pollution: open burning, landfill gases, bad smell
- Water pollution: leachate entering groundwater/surface water
- Soil pollution: plastics, metals, chemicals, pathogens

Hazardous portion of MSW:

- Hospital/biomedical waste is hazardous because it may carry pathogens and infectious materials.

Biomedical waste:

- More hazardous than ordinary waste because it can carry germs of communicable diseases.
- First step is segregation at source.
- Improper disposal can attract penalty; assignment key says fine and/or imprisonment of 5 years.

Biological decomposition:

- Dead organisms are broken into simpler compounds by bacteria, fungi, and other decomposers.
- This is essential for nutrient cycling.

### Bioremediation And Waste Reuse

Bioremediation uses living organisms, mainly microbes or plants, to reduce pollution.

| Method | Meaning | Best MCQ Clue |
|---|---|---|
| Natural attenuation | Natural pollutant reduction without major intervention | Slow, least controlled |
| Bioaugmentation | Adding specialized microbes | "Adding microbial strains" |
| Biostimulation | Adding nutrients/oxygen to stimulate native microbes | "Stimulate existing microbes" |
| Bioventing | Supplying air/oxygen to contaminated soil | Soil |
| Biosparging | Injecting air into saturated zone/groundwater | Groundwater |
| Biopiles | Excavated soil piled and aerated/treated | Controlled soil treatment |
| Composting | Aerobic decomposition of organic waste | Organic waste |
| Slurry-phase bioreactor | Soil/sludge mixed with water in reactor | Highly controlled, hotspots |

Heavy metals:

- Heavy metals cannot be biodegraded because they are elements.
- They can be transformed, immobilized, precipitated, adsorbed, or made less bioavailable.

Other assignment facts:

- Waste automobile tyres can be reused in low-cost passive house foundations/walls.
- Industrial wastes with high organic carbon can be used for energy recovery.
- Refillable glass and PET bottles save both energy and matter.
